# Writing Style Guide — Job Search Content

All agents generating resumes, cover letters, outreach messages, and thank-you notes MUST follow this guide. The goal: every piece of content should read like a confident professional wrote it on a Saturday morning, not like an AI generated it.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Words and Phrases That Scream AI
Never use these — they're the first things recruiters flag:

**Filler hedges:** "I believe", "I am confident that", "I am passionate about", "I am excited to", "I would love to"
**Corporate buzzwords:** "leverage", "utilize", "synergy", "spearhead", "innovative solutions", "cutting-edge", "best-in-class", "world-class"
**Vague amplifiers:** "significantly", "dramatically", "tremendously", "incredibly", "deeply"
**AI tells:** "delve into", "navigate the complexities of", "at the forefront of", "in today's rapidly evolving landscape", "I bring a unique blend of"

### Formatting Habits That Look AI-Generated
- **Excessive em dashes (—)** — one per paragraph max, zero is better. Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead.
- **Semicolons in every paragraph** — use them sparingly, one per page max
- **Parallel structure in every list** — real humans vary their sentence openings
- **Every bullet starting with a past-tense verb** — mix it up with context-first bullets
- **Perfectly balanced paragraphs** — real writing has short punchy paragraphs mixed with longer ones

## What Good Looks Like

### Resume Bullets
**Bad (AI-typical):**
> Spearheaded the development of an innovative AI-powered recommendation engine, dramatically improving user engagement by 45% and significantly reducing churn across the platform.

**Good (human-written):**
> Built the recommendation engine from scratch. Users who got personalized suggestions stayed 45% longer and churned at half the rate of the control group.

**Rules:**
1. Lead with WHAT you built, not HOW you "spearheaded" it
2. One metric per bullet, stated plainly. "45% longer" not "a remarkable 45% improvement"
3. If you have a comparison, state it: "half the rate" not "significantly reduced"
4. Short sentences. The period is your friend.
5. Vary structure: some bullets start with context ("When the team hit a scaling wall, I..."), some with action ("Shipped v2 in 6 weeks"), some with result ("Revenue grew 30% in Q3 after...")

### Cover Letters
**Bad opening (AI-typical):**
> I am writing to express my enthusiastic interest in the Senior Product Manager position at Affirm. With over 8 years of experience in product management and a deep passion for fintech innovation, I believe I would be an excellent addition to your team.

**Good opening (human-written):**
> The "Digital Wallets & Agentic Commerce" team name caught my eye because it's a product thesis, not a marketing headline. I've spent the last two years building exactly this: AI systems that help people make financial decisions they trust. Here's why Affirm is where I want to do it next.

**Rules:**
1. **First sentence**: Reference something SPECIFIC about the company or role. Not "I'm excited about your company" but "Your Series C announcement mentioned X, and that's exactly what I've been building."
2. **No "I am writing to express" openings.** Ever.
3. **Show, don't tell enthusiasm.** Don't say you're passionate. Describe what you built that proves you care.
4. **Three paragraphs max** after the opener: (1) your strongest relevant experience with a specific metric, (2) a second experience or a "why this company specifically" angle, (3) one sentence close
5. **Under 350 words total.** Recruiters spend 30 seconds on cover letters.
6. **End with a specific ask**, not "I look forward to hearing from you." Try: "I'd love to walk you through how I'd approach [specific challenge from JD]. Free for a 20-minute call this week?"

### Outreach Messages (LinkedIn, Email)
**Bad (AI-typical):**
> Hi Sarah, I hope this message finds you well. I came across your profile and was impressed by your experience at Anthropic. I am currently exploring opportunities in AI research and would love to connect and learn more about your team's work.

**Good (human-written):**
> Hi Sarah — I saw your talk on constitutional AI at NeurIPS. The bit about value alignment in production systems stuck with me because I ran into exactly that problem at Meta (we ended up building a human-in-the-loop review layer). I noticed the Principal UXR role on Anthropic's careers page. Would you be open to a quick chat about what research looks like on your team?

**Rules:**
1. **Reference a real connection point** — shared event, mutual contact, their public work, a blog post
2. **Keep it under 100 words** for cold outreach, under 150 for warm
3. **One ask, stated clearly.** "Would you be open to a 15-minute call?" not "I'd love to explore potential synergies."
4. **No "I hope this finds you well"** or "I wanted to reach out because"
5. **Mirror their style** — if their LinkedIn posts are casual, be casual. If formal, match it.

### Thank-You Notes
**Rules:**
1. **Reference a specific moment from the conversation** — "When you described the challenge with X, I started thinking about Y"
2. **Under 150 words.** This is not a second cover letter.
3. **Send within 4 hours** of the interview
4. **No new information** — just reinforce what went well and show you were listening

## Voice Principles

1. **Specific over general.** "I shipped 3 ML features in 6 months" beats "extensive experience in machine learning product development"
2. **Active voice.** "I built" not "was responsible for building"
3. **Short sentences for impact.** Save complex sentences for explanations, not claims.
4. **Contractions are fine.** "I've" and "we'd" sound more natural than "I have" and "we would"
5. **Vary paragraph length.** Mix 1-sentence punches with 3-sentence explanations.
6. **Read it out loud.** If you wouldn't say it in a conversation with a smart colleague, rewrite it.
7. **One em dash per page.** Use commas and periods instead.
8. **Cut "that" and "very" wherever possible.** Almost always unnecessary.

## Applying This Guide

Every content-generating skill (resume-tailor, cover-letter, hiring-manager-msg, connection-request, referral-request, thank-you-note) should:
1. Read this guide BEFORE generating content
2. After generating, self-review against the anti-patterns list
3. Rewrite any sentence that matches an anti-pattern
4. Verify the em dash count (max 1 per page)
5. Read the opening sentence — does it reference something SPECIFIC?
